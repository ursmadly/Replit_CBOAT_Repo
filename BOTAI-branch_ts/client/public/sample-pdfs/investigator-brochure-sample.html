1. Summary

GZT-34 is a novel selective glucagon-like peptide-1 (GLP-1) receptor agonist being developed for the treatment of type 2 diabetes mellitus. This Investigator's Brochure provides an overview of the relevant information available on GZT-34, including data on its pharmacology, pharmacokinetics, safety, and clinical experience to date.

2. Introduction

GZT-34 is an investigational compound that activates the GLP-1 receptor, leading to enhanced glucose-dependent insulin secretion, decreased glucagon secretion, delayed gastric emptying, and increased satiety. These effects contribute to improved glycemic control and weight loss in patients with type 2 diabetes.

2.1 Chemical Structure and Formulation

GZT-34 is a synthetic peptide analog of human GLP-1 with several modifications that extend its half-life and improve its resistance to degradation by DPP-4. The compound is formulated as a clear, colorless solution for subcutaneous injection, available in pre-filled pens containing 1.5 mg/mL of active ingredient.

3. Preclinical Studies

3.1 Pharmacology

In vitro studies have demonstrated that GZT-34 binds to the human GLP-1 receptor with high affinity (Ki = 0.3 nM), exhibiting approximately 5-fold higher potency than native GLP-1. In preclinical models, GZT-34 has shown the following effects:

  • Glucose-dependent stimulation of insulin secretion
  • Inhibition of glucagon secretion
  • Delay of gastric emptying
  • Reduction in food intake and body weight
  • Preservation of Î²-cell mass and function in animal models of diabetes

3.2 Pharmacokinetics

In animal studies, GZT-34 demonstrated the following pharmacokinetic properties:

  • Terminal half-life: 28-35 hours across species (rat, dog, monkey)
  • Bioavailability after subcutaneous administration: 65-78%
  • Minimal metabolism, with renal filtration as the primary elimination route
  • No significant inhibition or induction of cytochrome P450 enzymes

3.3 Toxicology

Comprehensive toxicology studies have been conducted in rats (up to 26 weeks) and monkeys (up to 39 weeks). Key findings include:

  • No treatment-related mortality at doses up to 50 times the anticipated human dose
  • Expected pharmacologic effects (reduced food intake, decreased body weight)
  • Minimal injection site reactions that resolved without intervention
  • No evidence of genotoxicity in standard assays
  • No carcinogenicity signals in 2-year rat studies
  • No adverse effects on fertility or embryo-fetal development in rats or rabbits

4. Clinical Experience

4.1 Phase 1 Studies

Three Phase 1 studies have been completed, including single ascending dose (SAD) and multiple ascending dose (MAD) studies in healthy volunteers and a 14-day study in patients with type 2 diabetes.

4.1.1 Safety and Tolerability

GZT-34 was generally well-tolerated at doses up to 3.0 mg once daily. The most common adverse events were:

  • Nausea (27% vs. 7% placebo)
  • Vomiting (12% vs. 2% placebo)
  • Headache (15% vs. 12% placebo)
  • Injection site reactions (8% vs. 5% placebo)

Gastrointestinal events were generally mild to moderate in intensity, transient, and decreased in frequency with continued dosing. No serious adverse events were considered related to the study drug.

4.1.3 Pharmacodynamics

In the 14-day study in patients with type 2 diabetes, GZT-34 demonstrated:

  • Dose-dependent reductions in fasting plasma glucose (-18 to -54 mg/dL)
  • Reductions in 2-hour postprandial glucose (-25 to -87 mg/dL)
  • Weight reduction of 0.5-2.1 kg after 14 days

4.2 Phase 2 Studies

A 12-week dose-ranging study has been completed in 285 patients with type 2 diabetes inadequately controlled on metformin monotherapy. Patients were randomized to receive placebo or GZT-34 at doses of 0.3, 0.6, 1.2, or 1.8 mg once daily.

4.2.2 Safety

The safety profile was consistent with the known effects of GLP-1 receptor agonists. The most frequent adverse events were:

  • Nausea: 8% (placebo), 16% (0.3 mg), 24% (0.6 mg), 31% (1.2 mg), 34% (1.8 mg)
  • Vomiting: 2% (placebo), 5% (0.3 mg), 9% (0.6 mg), 13% (1.2 mg), 15% (1.8 mg)
  • Diarrhea: 5% (placebo), 8% (0.3 mg), 11% (0.6 mg), 14% (1.2 mg), 16% (1.8 mg)

Most gastrointestinal events were mild to moderate and decreased over time. Discontinuation rates due to adverse events were 2% (placebo), 3% (0.3 mg), 5% (0.6 mg), 8% (1.2 mg), and 9% (1.8 mg).

4.3 Ongoing Clinical Studies

Two Phase 3 studies are currently ongoing:

  • Study GZT-3001: A 52-week efficacy and safety study in patients with type 2 diabetes on background metformin therapy
  • Study GZT-3002: A 26-week study comparing GZT-34 to insulin glargine in patients with type 2 diabetes inadequately controlled on oral antidiabetic agents

5. Summary of Risks and Benefits

5.1 Potential Risks

  • Gastrointestinal adverse events (nausea, vomiting, diarrhea)
  • Hypoglycemia (mainly when used with sulfonylureas or insulin)
  • Injection site reactions
  • Potential risk of pancreatitis (theoretical class effect, not observed to date)

5.2 Potential Benefits

  • Improved glycemic control
  • Weight reduction
  • Once-daily administration
  • Potential Î²-cell preservation

6. Guidance for Investigators

6.1 Dosing and Administration

For the PRO001 clinical trial, GZT-34 will be initiated at 0.3 mg once daily for 2 weeks, followed by dose escalation to 0.6 mg once daily. Depending on glycemic response and tolerability, further dose escalation to 1.2 mg may be considered after an additional 2 weeks.

6.2 Risk Mitigation

To minimize gastrointestinal adverse events, patients should be advised to:

  • Start at the lowest dose and escalate as per protocol
  • Eat smaller meals
  • Stop eating when feeling full
  • Avoid high-fat meals

6.3 Monitoring Recommendations

  • Monitor blood glucose regularly, especially when initiating therapy
  • Consider dose reduction of concomitant insulin or sulfonylurea to reduce hypoglycemia risk
  • Assess for signs and symptoms of pancreatitis at each visit
  • Monitor renal function periodically
